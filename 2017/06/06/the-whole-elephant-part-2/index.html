The whole elephant: Part 2

2017/06/06

Sometimes even psychologists forget about behavior.

- What people do, say.
- Internal processes (physio, genetic, feelings, thoughts, attitudes) important
   - Don't need to be neo-behaviorists, ignore internal processes
   - But, how do internal processes influence behavior?
   - Trend in neuroimaging toward brain/behavior relations a good one
- External (social: family/neighborhood/culture, and non-social: climate, environment) factors important
   - How ALSO affect behavior?
- Many psychologists are already there, others a modest but important conceptual shift in emphasis.
  1. Need bigger (and better) data about behavior
    • Why bigger?
      • Stochastic “laws”
      • Many small effects
      • Meaningful estimates of small, stochastic effects => bigger data
    • Why better?
      • Behavior spatially, temporally dense; contextually influenced, multidimensional
      • Big data not always signal, could be noise
      • Most psychological data are from WEIRD populations
      • Make assumptions about behavior, but don’t test them rigorously.
        • Do kids look at moms? No.
        • Do kids have to crawl before they walk? No.
        • Do kids make dumb mistakes about object sizes? Yes. (DeLoache)
        • Can you toilet train kids before 2-3 years. Yes.
  2. Video provides bigger and better data about behavior
    • How bigger?
      • Denser in time, space, storage demands, …
    • How better
      • Video provides better documentation about research procedures
    • Make video-recording a pillar of psychological science
      • Videos of behaviors, videos of procedures

  3. Exploiting video’s virtues requires infrastructure

    • Tools for dissecting video-recorded behavior
      • Unpack behavior
        • Micro: steps, eye movements
        • Meso: mood, prosody, speech/utterances
        • Macro: ???
      • Link with other streams
    • Tools to accumulate, share video + other streams
      • Databrary
    • Policies to permit sharing, linkage, protect participants
    • Future infrastructure for active discovery
      • Video data reuse
      • Searching, filtering, indexing, sampling, custom collections
      • Track provenance, versioning
      • From data library to Databservatory
        • Bring analyses to data, not data to analyses
        • Exploit deep learning, etc.
        • Dynamic models, etc.

  4. Changes in practices and attitudes

    • Data are more valuable than findings.
      • New techniques allow flexible stopping rules, findings to accumulate
    • We don’t own data, but we should (with permission) share it
      • Start with data underlying statistical analyses
      • Move toward raw data, including videos
    • Collecting new data is great (if you share it), but so is secondary analyses (if you share those)
      • Subfields of social science where secondary use is valued
    • Methods and materials may be more valuable than findings
      • Enables replication
    • How do my data fit into a broader whole?
      • Lego/Duplo bricks, electronic components, other standardized pieces allow complex structures to be designed and built.
        • Not one size fits all, but
        • Irregular stones require more mortar
      • To build complex theoretical structures to explain behavior may require us or push us toward more standardization, or at the very least, give more thought about links among data/concepts and measures.
        • Build conceptual ontologies around behavior
        • Example of video-recordings as code definitions
      • Linking variables in psychology
        • People
        • Places/context
        • Dates/time
        • Task/behavior
        • Measures
    • Adoption of open transparent, science practices as norm, not exception
      • Beyond badges

  5. Conclusion

    • Don’t have to agree with President Trump to acknowledge the potential power of mobilizing people with a simple catch-phrase.
    • Psychology is already really good, but we can make it great. Yes, we can. Shall we?
